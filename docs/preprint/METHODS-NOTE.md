## Abstract

Screening-level corrosion prediction underpins early-stage materials selection and
integrity decisions, yet the established tools are closed: their equations,
calibration data, and validation decks are proprietary, and they typically report
point estimates rather than quantified uncertainty. This makes published
predictions impossible to reproduce, audit, or extend. We present PitCast, an
open-source (Apache-2.0), browser-based corrosion-screening tool, together with a
fully reproducible benchmark built only on cited, openly redistributable measured
data. A pitting critical-temperature (CPT) correlation, recalibrated on the open
Nyby et al. (2021) dataset, achieves a leave-one-out mean absolute error (MAE) of
6.58 °C (RMSE 8.49 °C, n = 51, ASTM G48 / FeCl₃ basis) — at or below the
inter-laboratory reproducibility commonly reported for the G48 test it predicts —
and reports pitting probability through a Student-*t* prediction interval rather
than a point estimate. A five-model CO₂ corrosion ensemble is evaluated against
cited field and laboratory cases using an explicit model-disagreement (envelope)
metric that exposes where single-model point estimates are unsafe; over the
in-scope carbon-steel cases the envelope contains the measurement in only 33 % of
cases, an honest and decision-relevant warning that bulk-flowline models
over-predict where protective films dominate. The entire benchmark regenerates
from one command. The contribution is not greater raw accuracy than commercial
tools but a transparent, uncertainty-quantified, reproducible reference that closed
tools structurally cannot provide.

## 1. Introduction

Predicting corrosion behaviour at the screening level — ranking corrosion-resistant
alloys (CRAs) for chloride service, estimating carbon-steel CO₂ corrosion rates,
checking sour-service acceptability, assessing remaining strength of corroded pipe —
is a routine input to materials selection and asset-integrity work. The
correlations behind these tasks are well established and largely published:
de Waard–Milliams [1] and its 1995 revision [2] for sweet (CO₂) corrosion, the
NORSOK M-506 model [3], pitting-resistance-equivalent (PREN) ranking against the
ASTM G48 ferric-chloride test [4], and the ASME B31G family for corroded-pipe
remaining strength [5].

Despite this, the field lacks an **open, reproducible benchmark**. Three structural
gaps motivate this work:

1. **Closed tooling.** The widely used predictive packages are commercial and
   closed-source. Their internal equations, calibration datasets, and validation
   results are not disclosed, so a practitioner cannot audit *why* a number was
   produced or independently reproduce it.

2. **No open, reproducible benchmark.** Published model-comparison studies validate
   against proprietary or paper-specific datasets. As a survey of openly available
   measured-corrosion data confirms (Section 5; `benchmark/DATASET-SURVEY.md`),
   no central open repository of measured corrosion cases exists, and so
   model-comparison results in the literature generally cannot be reproduced or
   extended by a third party.

3. **Point estimates without uncertainty.** Screening outputs are commonly reported
   as single numbers. Yet the underlying correlations carry substantial,
   regime-dependent uncertainty, and different accepted models routinely disagree by
   an order of magnitude in the same service. A point estimate hides this.

This note describes a small but fully open and reproducible benchmark, distributed
with the open-source PitCast tool, and argues that **transparency combined with
quantified uncertainty** is a distinct and useful contribution that is independent
of — and not in competition with — the raw accuracy of closed commercial tools. We
deliberately do not claim to predict corrosion more accurately than those tools;
the claim is that an open, glass-box, uncertainty-first reference fills a role the
commercial tools cannot, because disclosing equations and calibration data would
dissolve their proprietary moat.

PitCast is explicitly a **screening-grade research and portfolio artifact**. It is
not validated for final design, is not an industrial platform, and is not a
substitute for alloy- or heat-specific qualification testing. Its intended use is
early-stage screening, education ("show your work"), and as a transparent
sanity-check reference.

## 2. Methods

### 2.1 Design principle: a glass-box, reproducible tool

PitCast is a self-contained, in-browser application: each engine is a vanilla
JavaScript port of a published, closed-form correlation, with the governing
equation, primary citation, calibration provenance, and uncertainty surfaced in the
output rather than hidden. There is no server-side model and no opaque
machine-learning layer. Every quantitative result is intended to be defensible from
the interface alone — the user can read the equation that produced it, the source it
came from, the data it was calibrated on, and the uncertainty attached to it.

The accompanying benchmark (`benchmark/run.js`) is a single Node.js script that
operates only on cited, in-repository data — the measured-CPT dataset
(`data/measurements.json`), the cited CO₂ and CRA cases (`data/validations.json`),
and the structured, scope-tagged CO₂ inputs (`benchmark/co2-inputs.json`). It
re-derives the CPT correlation independently, runs the leave-one-out analysis, runs
all five CO₂ models per cited case, and regenerates the report (`benchmark/REPORT.md`).
No synthetic or fabricated data is used anywhere, and adding a new case requires a
primary citation (a DOI or a standard clause). The benchmark is therefore
**reproducible** by anyone with the repository and **extensible** by anyone with a
new cited case — properties no closed validation deck provides.

### 2.2 CPT correlation and its prediction interval

We model the pitting critical temperature on an ASTM G48 (6 % FeCl₃ immersion)
basis as a linear function of the nitrogen-weighted pitting-resistance equivalent,

$$\mathrm{PREN_{N30}} = \mathrm{Cr} + 3.3\,\mathrm{Mo} + 30\,\mathrm{N},$$

$$\mathrm{CPT} = a\cdot \mathrm{PREN_{N30}} + b,$$

with composition in weight percent. The coefficients are fit on the FeCl₃-immersion
subset of the open Nyby et al. (2021) dataset [6] (n = 51), giving
**a = 2.038, b = −32.73, R² = 0.834** (the benchmark re-derives slope 2.0382,
intercept −32.731; the published in-code constants are 2.038176 / −32.730883, a
match to four significant figures — verification by independent reproduction).

Pitting probability is reported not as a point estimate but as

$$P(\text{pit}) = P(\mathrm{CPT} < T_\text{service})$$

evaluated under a **Student-*t* prediction interval** with df = n − 2. The *t*
distribution (rather than a Gaussian) is used deliberately because at this sample
size it does not understate the tail probability. A chloride-concentration overlay
and a σ-phase degradation term (the latter informed by published TTT behaviour for
austenitic and super-austenitic alloys [13, 14]) are applied as screening
adjustments, and physical clamps bound the output (CPT floor −15 °C, ceiling
120 °C). The model is reported strictly on a G48/FeCl₃ basis; electrochemical
(1 M NaCl) CPT measurements are systematically higher and are flagged, not merged,
as Section 3 quantifies.

### 2.3 CO₂ corrosion ensemble and the model-disagreement view

Carbon-steel sweet corrosion is computed as an ensemble of five published models:
de Waard–Milliams 1975 [1]; de Waard, Lotz & Dugstad 1995 [2] (reaction +
mass-transfer resistance in series with F_pH, F_scale, F_glycol factors); NORSOK
M-506:2017 [3]; the NESC/Cassandra mechanistic model (Nyborg 2010) [7]; and the
Multicorp/FreeCorp mixed-film model (Nešić 2007) [8]. The models share a common
chemistry layer — fugacity correction, Crolet–Bonis in-situ pH [9], and Sun–Nešić
FeCO₃ scaling tendency [10] — so that differences between members reflect the
corrosion-rate correlations themselves rather than inconsistent inputs.

The headline output is deliberately **not a single consensus number**. Instead we
report the full per-model spread and an **envelope-coverage** metric: the fraction
of cited cases whose measured rate falls within the model min–max envelope. This
turns inter-model disagreement into an explicit, decision-relevant uncertainty band
and surfaces regimes where every accepted model is wrong in the same direction.

### 2.4 Worked-example (code-verification) engines

Two further engines are verified as exact reproductions of consensus standards
rather than statistically. The corroded-pipe remaining-strength engine reproduces
the ASME B31G-2012 family [5] (original 0.667·*dL* parabolic area, modified
B31G / RSTRENG 0.85·*dL* effective area, and the Folias bulging factor [11, 12]);
its embedded worked case reproduces ASME B31G-2012 Appendix B, Example 1. The
sour-service engine implements the ISO 15156 / NACE MR0175 decision logic [15],
returning the in-scope routing and governing envelope with each verdict carrying
its citation. For both, the standard *is* the oracle, so the correct acceptance
criterion is exact reproduction (to rounding) of the standard's equations and
decision tree, not a numeric error against field data.

## 3. Validation and results

All numbers in this section are regenerated by `node benchmark/run.js` and recorded
in `benchmark/REPORT.md` and `benchmark/results.json`.

### 3.1 CPT correlation — out-of-sample (leave-one-out) error

Refitting the correlation on every n − 1 subset and predicting the held-out alloy
gives, on the FeCl₃/G48 basis:

| Metric | Value |
|---|---|
| Leave-one-out MAE (FeCl₃/G48 basis, n = 51) | **6.58 °C** |
| Leave-one-out RMSE / bias (FeCl₃) | 8.49 °C / −0.01 °C |
| Leave-one-out MAE (all methods, n = 117) | 13.33 °C |
| Full-sample fit (FeCl₃) | slope 2.0382, intercept −32.731, R² 0.834 |
| CPT records in dataset / without composition | 117 / 0 |

For context, the inter-laboratory reproducibility of the ASTM G48 test is itself on
the order of ±10–15 °C `[AUTHOR TO ADD: primary citation for the G48 inter-lab
reproducibility figure — stated in the engine V&V record but not yet sourced to a
specific round-robin study]`, so a 6.58 °C leave-one-out error places the model at
or below the scatter of the measurement it predicts. The near-zero bias (−0.01 °C)
indicates the correlation is well-centred. Mixing electrochemical (1 M NaCl) CPT
data with the FeCl₃ basis inflates the all-method leave-one-out MAE to 13.33 °C
(n = 117), which quantifies the importance of a consistent test basis and justifies
reporting the model on a single basis.

The largest individual leave-one-out residuals occur at the high-PREN extreme — the
Ni-base alloy N06625 (PREN_N30 ≈ 65.6) is the single worst miss at −23.6 °C,
followed by S31266 (+19.5 °C) and S32050 (+18.4 °C) — consistent with a linear PREN
model losing fidelity for highly alloyed material. The extremes should therefore be
treated as ranking-only.

### 3.2 CPT spot-check against fully cited CRA anchor cases

Against six independently cited CRA anchor cases, the predicted-vs-measured CPT is:

| Alloy | Basis | Measured °C | Predicted (G48) °C | Δ °C |
|---|---|--:|--:|--:|
| 304 | G48/FeCl₃ (on-basis) | 3.8 | 8.0 | +4.2 |
| 316L | G48/FeCl₃ (on-basis) | 23.75 | 19.1 | −4.7 |
| 317L | G48/FeCl₃ (on-basis) | 28.9 | 30.6 | +1.7 |
| 2205 | G48/FeCl₃ (on-basis) | 40.0 | 43.4 | +3.4 |
| 904L | electrochemical NaCl (off-basis) | 54.5 | 40.0 | −14.5 |
| 254 SMO | electrochemical NaCl (off-basis) | 86.49 | 61.3 | −25.2 |

The **on-basis (G48/FeCl₃) MAE is 3.49 °C** over the four on-basis anchors
(304, 316L, 317L, 2205) [16, 18]. The two electrochemical-NaCl anchors
(904L, 254 SMO) [17] are shown but excluded from the metric: the model is on a G48
basis and the electrochemical method differs systematically, under-predicting by
14–25 °C exactly as expected. Disclosing rather than discarding these off-basis
rows is itself part of the honesty of the benchmark.

### 3.3 CO₂ ensemble and envelope coverage

The five models were run per cited case. Over the three in-scope carbon-steel cases,
the per-model errors are:

| Model | MAE (mm/y) | RMSE (mm/y) | bias (mm/y) | Role |
|---|--:|--:|--:|---|
| NORSOK M-506 | 2.16 | 2.71 | +1.55 | primary |
| de Waard 1995 | 2.80 | 3.35 | +2.80 | primary |
| NESC/Cassandra | 9.85 | 11.94 | +9.85 | secondary |
| FreeCorp | 153.86 | 232.44 | +153.86 | unreliable at HP/HT |
| de Waard–Milliams 1975 | 722.11 | 1218.13 | +722.11 | conservative ceiling only |

NORSOK (MAE 2.16 mm/y) and de Waard 1995 (MAE 2.80 mm/y) are the reliable members.
The 1975 de Waard–Milliams model diverges by orders of magnitude at high
temperature and pressure because it lacks a protective-scale term — a known
conservative-only behaviour, not a coding error, which is why it is retained solely
as an upper bound.

The **envelope coverage over the in-scope carbon-steel cases is 33 % (1 of 3)**.
Coverage is low for an honest and important reason: where protective FeCO₃ or FeS
films dominate, the measured rate falls *below* every bulk-flowline model. This is
visible in the per-case results:

| Case | Scope | Measured (mm/y) | Ensemble min…max | Spread | In envelope? | Best model |
|---|---|--:|--:|--:|:-:|---|
| Nešić 2007 — 5 % CO₂ flow-loop, 60 °C [8] | CS aqueous | 2.0 | 1.09…13.66 | 12.5× | yes | NORSOK |
| Top-of-line corrosion — Singer 2017 [20] | TLC (out of scope) | 3.2 | 1.13…31.15 | 27.6× | yes | DWM-1995 |
| Tengiz sour-sweet field — Kahyarian 2017 [19] | CS aqueous | 0.55 | 1.64…52.76 | 32.1× | no | NORSOK |
| HP/HT well, 175 °C / 100 bar — Nyborg 2007 [22] | CS aqueous | 0.8 | 1.59…2110.15 | 1328× | no | DWM-1995 |
| 13Cr OCTG threshold — Mishina 2014 [21] | CRA (out of scope) | 0.12 | 2.87…353.24 | 123× | no | DWM-1995 |

The two deliberately out-of-scope cases — a 13Cr CRA that passivates above ~120 °C
and a top-of-line condensing-water case — are included precisely to demonstrate the
tool's scope boundary: a bulk carbon-steel model getting these "wrong" is correct
behaviour, and the benchmark records it as such. The CO₂ benchmark is small
(n = 3 in-scope) and is therefore reported as **indicative, not definitive**.

### 3.4 Worked-example checks

The B31G engine reproduces ASME B31G-2012 Appendix B, Example 1
(24″ OD × 0.281″ WT X52 pipe, 10″ × 0.1″ defect): predicted safe operating pressure
P_safe ≈ 54.3 bar (~787 psi), matching the published example, with the intact-pipe
limit reducing exactly to the Barlow thin-shell equation [5]. The MR0175 engine
reproduces representative decisions from ISO 15156-3 / NACE MR0175 [15] — e.g. a
13Cr OCTG at 120 °C and 8 kPa H₂S routes to Annex A sub-clause A.19 as in-scope,
while a 2205 duplex at 30 kPa H₂S is correctly rejected against the 22Cr (A.24)
envelope limit. Each verdict carries its citation string; 18 of 41 Annex A
sub-envelopes are deliberately flagged `needs_review` with null thresholds rather
than reconstructed from memory (see Section 5).

## 4. Discussion

The contribution of this work is not that PitCast predicts corrosion more accurately
than OLI, Honeywell Predict, DNV, or any other commercial package. We make no such
claim and have no basis to. The contribution is a different *kind* of artifact:
**open, transparent, reproducible, and uncertainty-quantified** screening.

Three properties distinguish it from the closed alternatives, and each exploits a
limitation those tools cannot remove without undermining their own business model:

- **Glass-box transparency.** Every PitCast number exposes its governing equation,
  its primary citation, the data it was calibrated on, and its uncertainty. A
  commercial tool that opened its equations and calibration data to this degree
  would dissolve its proprietary advantage; for an open tool it is the entire point.

- **Open, reproducible calibration and benchmark.** The CPT correlation is
  recalibrated on a public dataset anyone can download and re-fit, and the whole
  benchmark regenerates from a single command on cited in-repository data. Closed
  validation decks are, by construction, not reproducible by a third party.

- **Uncertainty and model-disagreement as first-class outputs.** Rather than a
  point estimate, the CPT model reports a *t*-based prediction interval, and the CO₂
  ensemble reports the spread across five accepted models as the uncertainty band.
  The 33 % envelope coverage is not a failure to hide but a useful, decision-relevant
  signal: it tells the engineer exactly where single-model screening over-predicts
  (protective-film regimes) and should not be trusted alone.

The appropriate framing is therefore **complementary role, not competition**. A
commercial thermodynamic package and an open glass-box screening reference answer
different questions: the former gives a calibrated proprietary number for design;
the latter gives an auditable, reproducible, uncertainty-bounded number for
screening, teaching, and sanity-checking — including sanity-checking the commercial
tool's output. An open reference that can be cited, reproduced, and extended has
value independent of whether it is the most accurate predictor in any single regime.

## 5. Limitations

The limitations are stated plainly because they bound the claims directly.

- **Screening-grade, not for final design.** PitCast is a research and portfolio
  artifact for early-stage screening, education, and sanity-checking. It is not
  validated for final design, is not an industrial platform, and does not replace
  alloy- or heat-specific qualification testing or a formal materials-selection
  process.

- **Small cited CO₂ corpus.** The CO₂ benchmark has only three in-scope
  carbon-steel cases. It is indicative, not definitive. Expanding the cited measured
  corpus toward 150–300 cases across CO₂/CPT/sour service is the planned next step
  (`PLAN-differentiation.md`); every new case must carry a primary citation and is
  added one at a time, never fabricated. A survey of openly available
  measured-corrosion data (`benchmark/DATASET-SURVEY.md`) confirms such data is
  scarce and largely locked in individual papers or proprietary decks — which is
  precisely why an open benchmark is differentiating but also why it grows slowly.

- **Self-validation pending external peer review.** The benchmark is built and run
  by the author. The leave-one-out protocol provides genuine out-of-sample error,
  and the worked examples are independently checkable against the cited standards,
  but the work has not yet undergone external peer review. Inviting that scrutiny —
  via this note and the open data — is an explicit goal.

- **CPT model basis and extremes.** The CPT correlation is reported on a single
  ASTM G48 / FeCl₃ basis; electrochemical-NaCl measurements differ systematically
  (Section 3.2) and are flagged, not merged. The correlation is linear in PREN_N30,
  so residuals grow at the high-PREN extreme (the worst leave-one-out miss is
  −23.6 °C for the Ni-base alloy N06625); the extremes should be treated as
  ranking-only. Crevice corrosion and non-aqueous regimes are out of scope.

- **CO₂ model scope.** The CO₂ ensemble is for bulk carbon-steel flowline service.
  It is not a top-of-line-corrosion model, does not apply to CRAs, and does not
  capture sour cracking; where protective films dominate it over-predicts (and the
  envelope metric flags this).

- **Standards currency.** Each engine's implemented standard edition is tracked
  explicitly (`docs/STANDARDS.md`). All load-bearing corrosion engines are current
  as of 2026-05; the one tracked delta is API RP 581, where the detailed-RBI damage
  factors are on a 3rd-edition basis while a 4th edition (2025) exists, and outputs
  are labelled accordingly.

- **MR0175 envelope completeness.** 18 of 41 Annex A sub-envelopes are flagged
  `needs_review` with null thresholds rather than reconstructed from memory; a
  `needs_review` verdict is not a qualified result and must be checked against the
  hard-copy standard.

## 6. Data and code availability

PitCast is open-source under the Apache-2.0 licence and is available at
<https://pitcast.austenite.org>. The public source repository is at
`[AUTHOR TO ADD: public GitHub repository URL]`.

All results in Section 3 are reproduced by running `node benchmark/run.js`, which
regenerates `benchmark/REPORT.md` and `benchmark/results.json` from the cited
in-repository data: `data/measurements.json` (the Nyby et al. 2021 dataset, 729
records, CC-BY [6]), `data/validations.json` (the cited CO₂ and CRA anchor cases),
and `benchmark/co2-inputs.json`. Per-engine verification and validation records are
in `docs/vv/`, and the standards-edition register is in `docs/STANDARDS.md`. A
`CITATION.cff` file is provided; a versioned, citable archive will be deposited on
Zenodo at first release (metadata in `.zenodo.json`), and the resulting DOI will be
added here.

No synthetic or fabricated data is used. Adding a new benchmark case requires a
primary citation (DOI or standard clause).
